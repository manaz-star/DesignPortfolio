01Lesson Objective
Why This Lesson Matters
Students will learn what a checking account is, how to use one correctly, and how to compare banking options to find what works for their specific needs. They will practice filling out a check, research real bank account features, and participate in a simulation that puts them in the role of both the customer and the institution. By the end, they will be able to make an informed decision about where and how to open a checking account.
01Define what a checking account is and explain its purpose in managing daily money.
02Demonstrate how to correctly fill out a check, including all required fields.
03Compare checking account options using real-world criteria like fees, access, and overdraft policies.
04Evaluate which banking tools best support different financial needs and habits.
05Practice critical thinking and communication through a structured roleplay simulation.
06Understand when a check may be more appropriate than a debit card or digital payment.
02Questions This Lesson Answers
  • ?What is a checking account and how does it actually work?
  • ?How do you fill out a check without making a mistake?
  • ?What should I compare when choosing a checking account?
  • ?When is using a check smarter than using a card?
  • ?How do my spending habits influence which bank is right for me?
  • ?What questions should I ask before I open any account?

04Lesson Flow
Opener Warm-Up Discussion: How Do You Choose? 10 min
Before introducing banking, students connect to a decision-making process they already know. This primes their thinking for the comparison activity that follows.
  • Set the frame: tell students that today's lesson is about checking accounts and how to choose the right one. Before we get there, we are going to talk about how we make any big purchase decision.
  • Ask students: "Say your phone broke this morning and you needed a replacement today. How would you figure out which one to get?" Let them call out answers freely. Do not redirect yet.
  • Bridge to banking: "That same process, comparing options, asking the right questions, looking at what matters to you personally, is exactly how you should approach picking a bank account."
Instructor NoteCommon responses will include going with the same brand, asking friends, or Googling. All of these are valid starting points. Use whatever they offer to connect to the research activity later in the session.
Direct Instruction What Is a Checking Account? 15 min
Students build a working understanding of what a checking account is, how money moves in and out of it, and the ways they can access their funds in real life.
  • Ask students to define a checking account in their own words before any instruction is given. Take 2 or 3 responses. Do not correct yet.
  • Introduce the working definition: a checking account is a bank account designed for daily transactions. It lets you deposit and withdraw money easily. It typically comes with a debit card, check access, and online banking.
  • Walk through the ways money can be accessed: visiting a branch, ATM withdrawal with a debit card, swiping a debit card at a store, paying online, setting up direct transfers or bill pay, using mobile payment apps like Apple Pay, Venmo, or Cash App, and writing a check.
  • Ask the group: "When might writing a check be better than using a card?" Discuss briefly. Common answers include paying rent, sending money by mail, or when a card is not accepted.
Instructor NoteSome students may not have a bank account or may come from unbanked households. Keep the tone exploratory rather than assumptive. The goal is to build financial literacy from wherever each student is starting.
Hands-On Activity Fill It Out: Blank Check Template 15 min
Students practice completing a check correctly using a real template. This is a foundational life skill that many students have never been explicitly taught.
  • Distribute the Blank Check Template to each student.
  • Ask a volunteer to read the directions aloud before anyone begins writing.
  • Give students 8 to 10 minutes to complete the check independently. Circulate and assist as needed.
  • Review the answers together as a class. Walk through each field: date, payee, numerical amount, written amount, memo line, and signature. Correct common errors in real time.
Instructor NoteThe most common errors are the written dollar amount line and forgetting the memo. Walk through the amount line slowly and show students how to fill to the end of the line so nothing can be added after the fact.
Research Activity Comparing Checking Accounts 20 min
Students research a real bank account from a provided list, verify their findings using two sources, and present their findings to the class. This builds research habits alongside financial literacy.
  • Distribute the Comparing Checking Accounts Activity Sheet, 4 pages total.
  • Each student selects a bank from the list on the worksheet and uses their device to research the account features.
  • Students verify all information using at least two sources before recording anything.
  • Once complete, each student presents their findings to the class in 1 to 2 minutes.
  • As a group, discuss: "Based on everything we heard today, which account seems like the best fit for most of us, and why?"
Instructor NotePush students past the homepage. Have them find the fee disclosure page, the overdraft policy, and whether mobile deposit is available. Those are the details that reveal which accounts are actually student-friendly.
Simulation Bank Battle: The Checking Account Showdown 25 min
Students step into roles as banks and customers in a fast-moving simulation. Banks compete for business by showcasing their features. Customers ask informed questions and choose the account that best fits their profile. This is where the lesson comes alive.
  • Divide the class in half. One half becomes banks. The other half becomes customers.
  • Distribute materials: banks receive a Bank Profile Sheet and 4 to 5 Feature Cards to display. Customers receive a Customer Profile Card and a Note Sheet to compare banks.
  • Set expectations: banks set up like a career fair and highlight their strongest features. Customers move around the room, visit at least three banks, and take notes at each stop.
  • Rotation rounds: customers spend 3 minutes at each bank. They ask questions like "What are your monthly fees?", "Do you offer mobile deposit?", and "What happens if I overdraft?" Banks answer based on their profile cards only.
  • After rotations, each customer chooses their bank and explains their decision to the group.
Sample Customer Profile
Jordan works part-time and gets paid every two weeks. She needs mobile deposit, low or no fees, and overdraft protection. She almost never visits a physical branch.
Sample Debrief Prompt
What made you choose that bank? Was there one feature that was a dealbreaker? Did anything surprise you about what different banks actually offer?
Instructor NoteRemind students they are choosing for their customer profile, not for themselves personally. This is an important distinction. It builds the habit of evaluating options based on need rather than brand recognition or what a friend or family member uses.
  • ›What feature mattered most to your customer? Did any bank deliver on it?
  • ›Was there anything a bank offered that you did not think to ask about?
  • ›How would your choice change if your customer profile were different?
  • ›What questions do you wish you had asked during the rotation?
Closing Wrap-Up and Transfer 5 min
Students consolidate what they learned and connect it to the broader skill of comparing options before making any major financial decision.
  • Recap the session: today students learned how to fill out a check, what a checking account is and how to access money from one, how to research and compare accounts using real criteria, and how to ask the right questions when evaluating any financial product.
  • Close with two questions for open discussion: "What other financial decisions in your life would benefit from this same comparison process?" and "After today, what is one thing you would do differently before opening any bank account?"

01Module Overview
What This Module Does
A "no" from a customer is not the end of a conversation. It is information. This module teaches sales associates how to hear a customer's objection clearly, respond with confidence rather than pressure, and keep the relationship open for what comes next. Learners work through a realistic customer scenario, make choices in the moment, receive immediate feedback, and leave with a three-step framework they can use the same day.
01Recognize the difference between a hard no and a not yet in a customer conversation.
02Apply the three-step Not Yet Framework to respond to objections without pressure.
03Choose responses in a branching scenario that keep the customer relationship intact.
04Identify what follow-up actions signal professionalism and build long-term trust.

Title / Hook
Turning a No Into a Not Yet
Time

On-Screen Content

Full-screen headline: "The customer just said no. Now what?"

Subhead: "What you do in the next 30 seconds determines whether you lose the sale or simply delay it."

Single call-to-action button: Start

Voiceover Script

"Every sales associate hears no. The ones who build lasting customer relationships know something the others don't: most no's are not permanent. They're a pause. In this module, you'll learn how to hear that pause, respond with confidence, and keep the door open. Let's get into it."

Visual Direction
VisualSplit-screen: left side shows a deflated sales associate; right side shows the same associate calm and composed. Clean, minimal. Dark background with warm accent type.
AudioSubtle ambient sound fades in. Voiceover begins at 3 seconds.

02
Concept Introduction
No Is Not the End. It's Data.
Time
2 min
On-Screen Content

Three labeled cards appear one at a time:

  • ·"I'm not ready right now." — timing objection
  • ·"That's more than I planned to spend." — budget objection
  • ·"I need to think about it." — confidence objection

Below: "Each of these is a not yet. Each one tells you something."

Voiceover Script

"When a customer says no, they're usually telling you one of three things: the timing is off, the price feels like a risk, or they don't feel confident enough yet. None of those are a closed door. They're clues. Your job is to hear which one it is and respond to that, not to the word no."

Visual Direction
AnimationCards animate in one at a time on click or timed 2-second intervals. Each card pulses briefly when it appears.
Design NoteUse icon + label pairing. Clock icon for timing, dollar icon for budget, question mark for confidence. Keep it clean, no clutter.
Slide
03
Framework Introduction
The Not Yet Framework
Time
2 min
On-Screen Content

Three-step visual sequence:

  • 01
    Acknowledge. Validate what the customer said without agreeing that it's over.
  • 02
    Clarify. Ask one question to understand the real reason behind the no.
  • 03
    Offer a path. Propose one next step that removes pressure and keeps the relationship open.
Voiceover Script

"The Not Yet Framework has three steps. First, acknowledge what the customer said. Not in a way that concedes the sale, but in a way that shows you heard them. Second, ask one clarifying question. One. Not a list. Find out what's actually underneath the no. Third, offer a path forward that removes pressure. You're not pushing. You're opening a door."

Visual Direction
AnimationThree steps build sequentially. Each step number animates in with a brief color highlight before settling. Step text appears after the number.
Design NoteFramework should feel like a tool, not a lecture. Consider a horizontal progression arrow connecting the three steps.
Slide

Scenario Setup
Meet the Customer: Marcus
Time
1 min
On-Screen Content

Customer profile card:

  • ·Name: Marcus
  • ·Situation: Browsing a furniture showroom, considering a living room set
  • ·Budget: Has not shared one
  • ·Body language: Engaged, asking questions, but hesitating at the price tag

"I appreciate the time, but I don't think this is going to work for me right now."

Voiceover Script

"This is Marcus. He came in asking questions, he's been engaged for the last 15 minutes, and now he's pulling back. He just told you it's not going to work for him right now. You have a choice about what you do next. Let's see how you handle it."

Visual Direction
VisualIllustrated customer profile card. Neutral illustrated character, not a photo. Warm showroom background. Marcus's dialogue appears in a speech bubble, slightly stylized.
TransitionPause 1 second after voiceover ends before advancing to the branching slide.

05
Branching Scenario
What Do You Say Next?
Time
3 min
On-Screen Content
Choose Your Response

Marcus says: "I appreciate the time, but I don't think this is going to work for me right now."

Learner selects one of three responses:

Option A "I completely understand. Is it the price, or is the timing just not right? I'd love to find something that works for you."
Option B "We do have a payment plan if that helps. And this set is only available at this price for two more days."
Option C "No problem at all. Here's my card if you change your mind."
Voiceover Script

"Marcus just gave you a not yet. You have three choices. Think about what you learned in the framework. Which response acknowledges what he said, clarifies the real objection, and offers a path forward without pressure? Choose one."

Visual Direction
InteractionThree clickable response tiles. On hover, each tile highlights. On selection, the module branches to the appropriate feedback slide.
Design NoteDo not label options as correct or incorrect before selection. Let the learner commit to a choice before receiving feedback.
Slide
06
Branching Feedback
Here's What That Response Did
Time
2 min
On-Screen Content

If Option A selected (correct):

You acknowledged Marcus without conceding the sale, then asked one clarifying question. That is the framework in action. Marcus now has space to tell you what's actually going on. Well done.

If Option B selected:

You moved to urgency and payment plans before understanding the real objection. Marcus may feel pressured. That response skipped the clarify step entirely.

If Option C selected:

You were polite but you closed the door yourself. Handing over a card with no follow-up question signals you accepted the no. Try again.
Voiceover Script

For Option A: "That's the Not Yet Framework. You heard Marcus, you didn't panic, and you asked the one question that opens the conversation back up. That's what a not yet response looks like."

For Options B and C: "Let's look at what happened there and why it matters. Then we'll give you a chance to try again."

Visual Direction
Feedback DesignCorrect response: green accent border, checkmark icon, warm encouraging tone. Incorrect: amber accent border, no harsh red, constructive language only. Retry button appears for incorrect selections.
Branching LogicCorrect path advances to Slide 7. Incorrect paths loop back to Slide 5 with a brief hint visible on the response tiles.
Slide
07
Application
Putting the Framework to Work
Time
3 min
On-Screen Content

Three real-world objection examples with the framework applied to each:

  • "I need to think about it."
    Acknowledge: "Of course, this is a real decision."  |  Clarify: "Is there a specific part you're still weighing?"  |  Offer: "I can put together a summary so you have something to reference."
  • "That's out of my budget."
    Acknowledge: "I hear you."  |  Clarify: "What range were you working with?"  |  Offer: "Let me show you a few options that might get you closer."
  • "I'm not ready yet."
    Acknowledge: "No rush at all."  |  Clarify: "Is there a timeline you're working toward?"  |  Offer: "I'll make a note and follow up when the timing makes more sense."
Voiceover Script

"Here is how the framework sounds in three common situations. Each one starts the same way: acknowledge first, ask one question second, offer a path third. Notice that none of these responses push. They create space for the customer to keep talking. That is the difference between a lost sale and a delayed one."

Visual Direction
AnimationEach objection example builds on click. First the customer statement, then the three-step response unfolds beneath it. Learner controls the pace.
Design NoteUse a consistent visual structure for each example so the pattern becomes familiar. Consistency here reinforces the framework.
Slide
08
Knowledge Check
Let's See What You've Got
Time
3 min
Questions
1) A customer says "I just need more time to think." According to the Not Yet Framework, what should your first response be?
  • ATell them about the current promotion so they feel motivated to decide now.
  • BAcknowledge what they said, then ask one question to understand what they're still weighing.
  • CGive them your card and let them reach out when they're ready.
2) Which of the following is the correct order of the Not Yet Framework?
  • AOffer a path, clarify the objection, then acknowledge the customer.
  • BClarify the objection, acknowledge the customer, then offer a path.
  • CAcknowledge, clarify, offer a path.
3) A customer says "That's more than I was planning to spend." What is the most effective clarifying question?
  • A"We do have financing available. Would that help?"
  • B"This is actually one of our best values at this price point."
  • C"What range were you working with? I'd like to see what we can do."
Design Notes
FeedbackImmediate feedback on each question. Correct answers highlighted in sage green. Incorrect selections show the correct answer with a brief explanation before advancing.
ScoringScore displayed at end: 3 of 3 advances to completion screen. Below 3 of 3 offers a review of the framework before retaking.
CompletionFinal screen: "You've got the framework. Now use it." Simple, direct. Option to download a one-page reference card.
